Using Triples to Harmonize Data

Triples can be used to harmonize data coming in from different systems.

For example, for a company name that is different in two different systems (perhaps the company trades under more than one name), a triple could be written to say that they are actually the same company.

<http://procycling.com/resource/Biking_Winery_Tours>
<http://www.w3.org/2002/07/owl#sameAs>
<http://procycling.com/resource/Napa_Biking_Tours>

Or, for an intelligence organization, triples could be used for different names that people have used (i.e. aliases), or even just variations of their name.

<http://organization.com/resource/Lord_Voldemort>
<http://www.w3.org/2002/07/owl#sameAs>
<http://organization.com/resource/Tom_Riddle>

Relationships and Facts

Triples are excellent for describing relationships between entities, and can also be used for facts about entities.

For example, a triple could say that Outback Rides was acquired by Sam Stone.

<http://procycling.com/resource/Outback Rides>
<http://procycling.com/ontology/acquiredBy>
<http://procycling.com/resource/Sam_Stone>

With a few triples similar to the one above, a query could then be written to see if there are other companies that have been acquired by Sam Stone.

Or, a triple could be written to say that a person is on the board of directors for an organization.

<http://procycling.com/resource/Rolling_Fast_Deliveries>
<http://procycling.com/ontology/boardMember>
<http://procycling.com/resource/Michael_Ross>

With a few triples similar to the one above, a query could then be written to see what other companies have Michael Ross as a board member.

Or, a triple can be used to simply specify a fact about an entity.

<http://procycling.com/ontology/acquiredIn>
